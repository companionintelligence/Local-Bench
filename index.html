Local Bench

Local Bench is a companion app that benchmarks local language models on your own hardware. It runs a set of prompts through models served by Ollama, measures how each one performs, and saves the results so you can compare them over time.

Everything runs locally. Your prompts and the model responses stay on your machine. Use the tabs on the left to learn what Local Bench shows, the kinds of prompts it runs, and how it helps you plan multi-agent systems.

What it shows, and why

Local Bench shows how a model actually behaves on your machine:

  • Throughput and timing for each model (how quickly it produces tokens).
  • Your system specs recorded next to every run, so a number always has context.
  • A history of runs, stored to disk, so you can compare before and after a change such as a new GPU, a different quantization, or a newer model.

Why it matters: a model that is fast on someone else's hardware may be too slow on yours. Before you build anything on top of a local model, Local Bench tells you what to expect, in real numbers, on the hardware you own.

Types of prompts

Local Bench runs a set of benchmark prompts rather than a single one, because different workloads stress a model in different ways. These categories map to how you will actually use a model:

  • Short prompts with short answers. Closest to quick, high-frequency calls. Latency matters most here.
  • Long-context prompts. Large inputs such as documents, transcripts, or chat history test how a model handles a full context window.
  • Reasoning or multi-step prompts. Longer generations where steady throughput and stability matter.

Pick the prompt types that match your real use case and weigh the results accordingly.

Building multi-agent systems

A multi-agent system runs several models, or several copies of a model, working together: one plans, others carry out steps, another reviews. On local hardware they all share the same GPU and memory, so performance is a budget you spend.

Local Bench helps you plan that budget:

  • Match models to roles. Put a small, fast model on high-frequency agents (routing, simple tool calls) and reserve a larger, slower model for the few steps that need deeper reasoning.
  • Know your ceiling. Throughput figures tell you roughly how many agents can run at once before the system slows down.
  • Re-test after changes. When you swap a model or change hardware, run the benchmark again so your agent design still fits the numbers.
  • Start small. Benchmark the one or two models you plan to use, confirm they are fast enough for the busiest agent in your system, then add more agents from there.
